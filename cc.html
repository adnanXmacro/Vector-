Projectile Motion — Reference

Time of Flight
t = (v0 sin θ + √(v02 sin2θ + 2g·h0)) / g
Total time airborne, from launch at height h0 to landing on flat ground. With h0 = 0, this simplifies to t = 2v0sinθ / g.
Maximum Height
H = h0 + (v02 sin2θ) / 2g
Peak height above the ground, reached when vertical velocity hits zero — at exactly half the flight time on flat terrain.
Maximum Range (flat ground)
R = (v02 sin 2θ) / g
Horizontal distance travelled. This is maximised at θ = 45° on level ground with no wind or drag.
Range on Sloped Ground
R = (2v02 cosθ sin(θ−α)) / (g cos2α)
Where α is the ground slope angle (positive = uphill). Firing uphill (α > 0) shortens the range; firing downhill (α < 0) extends it. The optimal launch angle also shifts away from 45°, toward 45° + α/2.
Velocity Components
vx = v0 cos θ     vy = v0 sin θ − gt
Horizontal velocity stays constant when there's no wind. Vertical velocity decreases linearly under gravity, crossing zero at peak height. (Enable Wind to add horizontal air resistance, which breaks the clean parabola below.)
Trajectory Equation
y = x·tanθ − (g·x2) / (2v02cos2θ)
The path is a parabola. This lets you find height y at any horizontal distance x directly, without solving for time first.
Two Points, Same Height
x1 + x2 = R   (symmetric about the peak)
On flat ground, any horizontal line below the apex crosses the parabola at two points equidistant from the range's midpoint. This symmetry breaks when wind or slope is introduced.
Impact Speed
vimpact = √(vx2 + vy2)
On flat ground with no drag, impact speed equals launch speed v0 — energy is conserved. Air resistance or landing above/below launch height changes this.
